The Underground booklet

5.43 × 8.27 in, 16 pages, glue-bound
2021

Fyodor Dostoevsky’s Notes from Underground (1864) reads as a frenetic stream of critical consciousness: the unnamed, embittered narrator frequently jumps from one thought to the next without transition, yet all his seemingly divergent thoughts ultimately relate back to his foundational critique of mid-19th-century Russian society and Enlightenment ideals of rationalism.

I designed the indentation of my booklet to reflect this breathlessness of the Underground Man’s narration. Though visually irregular, the text reads as one continuous stream: as you finish one paragraph, the next is already lined up right there ready for you to begin.

Aside from the rag right, the layout is largely modeled off of Enlightenment standards, specifically that of a 1782 edition of Rousseau’s Confessions, which the Notes parody.

For the typeface, I looked at a 1839 edition of The Sorrows of Young Werther, another epistolary novel which I felt was thematically resonant with the Notes. Its typeface closely matched New Clarendon, so I used that for the Notes.
